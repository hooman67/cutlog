## STEP 3 — Web Cross-Check of the Dominant Objection

**Question:** Is "every machine is different" so strong that shared data is worthless? And is Lobo's "speed is consistent across brands for a given wattage" claim supported?

**Finding (two-sided, with citations): The objection is real but does NOT invalidate shared data. The community's own position — "starting point, must test" — is correct and near-universal, endorsed even by LightBurn staff and by the shared databases themselves.**

### Lobo's "speed ≈ same across brands per wattage" claim: TRUE in weak/directional form, FALSE in strong form

- **Directional support:** Manufacturer settings tables ARE organized by wattage, and speed scales monotonically with power for a fixed material. Epilog's official CO2 chart is literally columned `50W / 60W / 80W / 100W / 120W`; for 6mm acrylic, speed runs 3/4/6/8/10% as wattage climbs (https://www.epiloglaser.com/support/material-settings/). Wikipedia's CO2 cut-rate table confirms strong, predictable material/thickness/power dependence (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laser_cutting). So "wattage + material is the dominant driver of speed" — the directional version of Lobo's claim — holds.
- **Strong form fails:** At ~55–60 W CO2 cutting acrylic, absolute speeds across brands span ~4–5x: Trotec Speedy 300 60W ≈ 18 mm/s (https://www.bonnycreations.com/settings/machines/trotec-speedy-300), xTool P2 55W ≈ 25 mm/s (https://3dwithus.com/xtool-p2-review-55w-co2-laser-cutter-tests-tips-and-settings), OMTech 60W ≈ 11.8 ipm ≈ 5 mm/s (https://mellowpine.com/omtech-60w-co2-laser-review/). So you cannot copy an absolute speed number across brands — but you CAN use it to land in the right order of magnitude and converge with a short test.

### Why machines genuinely differ (validates the operators)

- **Tube/source rating dishonesty even when new:** a "50W" tube measured 43W (~14% short); "a K40 rated at 40W… is only [the tube length] of a 30W device" (https://forum.lightburnsoftware.com/t/something-is-not-right-omtech-60w/135973). Diode "watts" are often input/electrical, not optical — discrepancy up to ~5x; "Most lasers have at most about 25% efficiency" (https://forum.lightburnsoftware.com/t/what-is-the-truth/53234).
- **Power % is non-linear and machine-specific:** "50% power based on a mA reading… is likely percentage current, not percentage of output power" (same OMTech thread). So "50%" ≠ same physical output on two machines.
- **Degradation over time** (validates Nate's 100%/5yr vs 85%/40yr point): a 3-yr-old 50W tube failing of "old age / usage" (https://forum.lightburnsoftware.com/t/need-help-power-output-stays-at-2-ma-regardless-of-power-setting/104979).
- **Material batch variation** (validates Matthew Olds / Michael Barry "sheet to sheet"): same nominal plywood needing a **5x speed change** between batches — "the laser just doesn't make it through unless I reduce speed to around 2 mm/s" vs 10 mm/s (https://forum.lightburnsoftware.com/t/new-batch-of-ply-wont-cut-whats-wrong-with-the-material/153130). Peer-reviewed mechanism: wood "porosity and density can vary… change local absorptivity" (MRS Bulletin 2026, https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13226462/).
- **Assist gas purity** (validates the industrial cutters): "A 1% reduction in the purity of oxygen can produce a 25% reduction in cutting speed" (Riveiro et al., Materials 2019, https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6337310/). Focus is often the single most decisive factor for N2 stainless cutting (Mahrle et al. 2021, https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8161254/) — matching Lobo's "find the right focus distance first."

### The "starting point, must test" consensus is near-universal — and so is the value of starting points

- LightBurn staff: manufacturer tables are "a great place to find **starting points** for testing" (https://forum.lightburnsoftware.com/t/ballpark-on-determining-the-material-cut-settings/131391); and "a material test is the best starting point" (https://forum.lightburnsoftware.com/t/settings-for-purchased-files/106423).
- Community: purchased/free libraries are "too inaccurate… at best a starting point" (https://forum.lightburnsoftware.com/t/comprehensive-material-library/98522).
- lasertips.org's own disclaimer: "These settings are provided as a **starting point only**… Always perform your own testing before production" (https://lasertips.org/).
- The cleanest transfer-failure mechanism: LightBurn stores power as **% of full power**, so even within the same brand/model a 20W vs 40W library produces different physical results unless normalized to **energy/length (J/mm)** (https://forum.lightburnsoftware.com/t/material-library-for-two-lasers-with-different-laser-watts/95066).

**Net:** Shared data is NOT worthless. It reliably collapses a blank-slate guess into a small, targeted test grid — which is exactly Nate's "90% there" and Tinker's "it's just speed at that point." But it is NOT plug-and-play, and the right technical framing is to **normalize to J/mm**, label entries as *tested starting points*, and pair them with a one-click material-test workflow. CutLog's positioning ("better starting point, fewer test squares") is defensible. Its positioning ("per-machine AI learns your drift and gives you THE number") overreaches what the physics and the operators support.

*Research caveat: primary fiber-source-maker cross-brand tables (IPG/Raycus/Precitec/Bodor) and Reddit/Glowforge/Etsy pages were blocked in this environment; the fiber cross-brand speed claim rests on CO2 analogues + peer-reviewed mechanisms, not a verified fiber table.*

## STEP 4 — Brutally Honest Assessment

### Does the feedback validate or invalidate the core value proposition?

**Partially validates the problem; substantially invalidates the original pitch.**

- The **problem is real and universal** — everyone burns material, everyone hates dialing in, everyone wants a better starting point. Pain severity is genuinely high (the indiehackers "10/10 pain" claim survives scrutiny).
- The **"per-machine AI that learns your machine's drift and gives you the right number" pitch is not supported.** Operators (and the physics) say the last-mile is irreducibly a material test on YOUR machine that day. Worse, the segment most engaged (Nate, galvo) explicitly says his machines *don't drift* and *don't use* most of the logged fields. The AI-personalization story is the weakest part of the proposition.
- What IS validated is the humble version: **a searchable, importable, community-sourced starting-point database that gets you to a smaller test grid faster.** Nate ("90% there"), Tinker ("just speed"), Michael Bergeron (Etsy files "good for starting points"), and LightBurn staff all endorse this exact framing.

### Which segment to target?

The strategy_pivot doc picked **industrial fiber cutting** as primary based on lead-gen (3 of 5 hot leads, highest cost-per-failure). The feedback supports the *pain* but reveals a **distribution/incumbency trap**:
- Industrial buyers get parameters **bundled at install + live OEM WhatsApp/WeChat support** (George Diffey, Les Strickland, Chris Savar). Sara Simpson won't even cut off-list. They are the highest-value but also the **best-served and most safety-conscious** ("ruined nozzles, cracked windows, back-reflection") — they will trust an OEM engineer over a stranger's logged data. Hardest to win on trust.
- The **galvo engraving segment** (Nate's world) is the most *engaged and evangelistic* (Nate offered 20K YouTube + 225K TikTok distribution) but has **low cost-per-failure** and **rejects the cutting-centric data model** (no gas, no drift, different vocabulary). Good for distribution, weak for the core value prop unless the product is re-tooled around line-interval/Q-pulse/frequency.
- **Hobbyist CO2/diode** is receptive and low-trust-barrier but lowest pain and zero willingness to pay.

**Honest read:** No segment is a clean winner. The most defensible wedge is **galvo/MOPA fiber engraving via Nate-style creator distribution** for *reach and feedback*, while building genuine depth in **industrial thick-material cutting starting points** for *value* — but monetization is unproven in both.

### Friction of getting people to LOG cuts / contribute data

**This is the existential problem.** The data shows it clearly:
- The contribution mechanism (.clb import) **didn't even work** for the one committed power user: Nate — *"Doesn't seem to recognise .clb files."*
- Even after fixes, logging is *tedious*: Nate — *"doing that hundreds of times will be quite tedious… That's just one laser and lens combination."*
- Every warm lead is chronically *time-poor*: Mike apologized for being busy 3+ times; Nate "a bit flat out… time poor"; John Stegenga "too much going on."
- The strategy doc itself conceded the chicken-and-egg: *"per-machine learning needs more feedback data… (chicken and egg)"* and pivoted to *"deliver value first, then prompt 'did this work? yes/no' (lowest friction)."* That pivot is correct.
- Operators already have a logging home they trust (LightBurn library + saved files + physical samples). Asking them to re-log into a second tool is pure friction with deferred payoff. **A community-data flywheel that depends on busy operators manually logging cuts is unlikely to spin.**

### Pivots / repositionings the feedback suggests

1. **Lead with the .clb importer as the wedge, not the AI.** It's the one feature with concrete pull (Klaus, Nate). Make it bulletproof for galvo formats. The value is "all your scattered settings searchable in one place" — a personal organizer first, community DB second.
2. **Drop / heavily soften the per-machine-AI-drift narrative.** It overreaches the physics and alienates galvo users whose machines don't drift. Reposition as "tested starting points + LightBurn material test = fewer test squares." Normalize stored data to **J/mm** so it transfers across wattages honestly.
3. **Pick ONE segment and go deep.** Spreading across 16 FB groups + 10 subreddits is the failure mode the pivot doc already flagged. Galvo engraving (creator-distributed) OR industrial cutting (depth) — not both.
4. **Reframe contribution as zero-effort.** Harvest data from .clb imports automatically (with consent) rather than asking for manual logging. The "did this work? yes/no" prompt is the right friction level.
5. **Be honest there is no monetization yet.** The free/no-paywall position has produced zero willingness-to-pay. A creator-affiliate or material-supplier-sponsorship model is more realistic than asking operators to subscribe.
6. **Treat the data flywheel as a bonus, not the moat.** The moat the operators actually respect is a *curated, verified, well-organized* starting-point library (like a better lasertips.org / OMG page with .clb import), not "AI that knows your machine."

## Bottom Line

Does real operator feedback support CutLog being a winner? **Not as currently pitched — but a narrower version is viable.** The pain is genuine and universal, and the web research confirms the operators are right that machines vary enough that no setting is plug-and-play (4–5x same-wattage speed spreads; tube-rating dishonesty; 1% O2 purity → 25% speed; 5x batch swings). But the SAME research confirms the operators' OTHER belief: a good starting point has real value and collapses the search to a quick test — Nate's "even getting people in the ballpark for material testing, that's 90% there." So the core idea isn't dead; the *AI-learns-your-machine* story is. CutLog's most defensible self is a **searchable, .clb-importing, J/mm-normalized library of tested starting points** that pairs with LightBurn's material test, not a magic per-machine oracle.

Best-chance segment + positioning: **galvo/MOPA fiber engraving, distributed through a creator like Nate (20K YT / 225K TikTok), positioned as "import your scattered LightBurn settings + get a tested starting point so you run fewer test squares."** Industrial cutting is higher-value but locked up by bundled OEM libraries and WhatsApp support, and those buyers won't trust strangers' data on $180 sheets. The hardest, possibly fatal, risk is the data flywheel: every warm lead is too busy to log, the importer broke on the one user who tried, and willingness to pay is zero. Win by making contribution automatic (harvest from imports) and proving value before asking for anything.
